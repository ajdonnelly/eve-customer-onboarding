About

I understand law-firm adoption from the inside

For more than a decade I ran the daily operations of a boutique plaintiff-side employment and corporate-law firm. I handled many elements of the practice end to end: intake, client communication, case and matter management, discovery, document production, vendor and software coordination, and billing. I know how a firm actually runs, where the most significant pain points are, and what it takes to get professionals to trust and adopt a new way of working.

Across my career, I have also been part of repeated waves of technology adoption and process improvement. Early on, that meant helping digitize hard-copy files and streamline billing workflows. At a larger firm, it included supporting the implementation of automated billing processes across a roughly 50-attorney practice. In plaintiff-side litigation, it meant working alongside firm leadership and colleagues to evaluate, pilot, and adopt new systems as the practice evolved — from billing software transitions such as WinVantage to Clio, to multiple litigation-platform changes, including Clio to Casefleet, and the move from PACER-based tracking toward a more automated digital docketing workflow through DocketBird.

More recently I have done that adoption work for AI. As a senior litigation paralegal I joined my firm’s AI committee and an enterprise pilot integrating tools like Microsoft Copilot and Filevine’s Lois into real legal workflows — testing them against live tasks (including close QC of AI-generated medical chronologies), evaluating output, documenting workflows, and co-leading training for colleagues from staff to partners. My graduate training in biomedical visualization is, at its core, about making complex things understandable. This fall I begin the University of Hawaiʻi Executive J.D. program focused on AI law and governance.

Adoption

Adoption is a learning problem, not a software problem

A firm doesn’t change how it works because a tool is powerful — it changes because its people are brought along deliberately. My approach draws on learning theory, adult training, and more than a decade inside firms: meet people in their real workflow, build understanding in the order people actually learn, and earn trust before asking anyone to rely on AI.

01

Start from the workflow

Anchor every rollout in how the firm actually runs a matter — not a generic feature tour.

02

Teach the way people learn

Role-based, scaffolded training with practice and reinforcement — not a one-time demo that fades by Friday.

03

Protect trust

Coach the review-against-source habit on every AI draft and extraction, so confidence is earned, not assumed.

04

Measure what matters

Tie adoption to time-to-value, utilization, and an outcome the firm actually cares about.

Why this work

Why legal AI, and why Eve

I am drawn to legal AI because I believe it is one of the most important ways to serve innovation in law firms now.

For all of the discussion about disruption, the real opportunity is not simply to introduce new technology into legal practice. It is to help firms use it in ways that reduce friction, expand their capacity, improve the quality and consistency of their work, and create more room for the human parts of practice that clients actually need: judgment, communication, strategy, advocacy, and care.

Having worked inside firms through multiple waves of technology adoption, I have seen how much potential can be lost when a tool is treated as the solution rather than as part of a larger change process. The most meaningful transformation happens when technology is paired with an understanding of the people doing the work, the information they are trying to manage, and the practical pressures they face every day.

That is why I see legal AI not as an abstract category or a temporary trend, but as a chance to help firms build more intelligent, responsive, and human-centered ways of working from the inside out.

Why Eve

Eve is especially compelling to me because it is building for a part of legal practice I know intimately: plaintiff-side firms trying to turn overwhelming volumes of information into stronger cases, better client service, and more time for real legal judgment.

For years, I have watched attorneys and staff confront the same underlying challenge from inside the firm: how do you make sense of thousands of pages of medical records, case documents, billing materials, communications, and deadlines without burying the people doing the work in repetitive administrative labor? How do you create systems that make a firm more organized, more responsive, and more effective without losing the human judgment that gives the work its value?

Medical chronologies are not an abstract use case. They are one of the most consequential and labor-intensive parts of plaintiff-side case development. They shape early case evaluation, damages analysis, expert preparation, mediation strategy, and ultimately the story a firm can tell about a client’s injury.

This is a problem I have tried to help solve from within firms through process improvement, technical skill, and emerging tools. Seeing Eve approach that problem directly — and seeing the level of enthusiasm users have expressed about the practical impact on their work — has made me especially excited about the possibility of contributing to the company.

What stands out to me is not simply the promise of producing work product faster. It is the opportunity to build technology around the actual pressure points of plaintiff practice: reviewing complex records, creating usable medical chronologies, supporting intake and case development, drafting materials, and helping firms move work forward without requiring every task to begin from a blank page.

I was particularly energized by hearing Eve customer feedback on Trial Lawyer Nation and by the excitement around what the platform could make possible for plaintiff firms. I recognized that reaction. I have seen what it feels like inside a firm when a technology genuinely removes friction, makes difficult work more manageable, and gives attorneys and staff more time to focus on the work only they can do.

That kind of impact matters to me because it is not merely operational. Better systems can help firms respond more quickly, organize complex information more effectively, strengthen the development of a case, and spend more time with clients during difficult periods in their lives.

I am also drawn to Eve’s broader multimodal potential. My background in biomedical visualization has made me deeply interested in how people learn from and make sense of complex information. The future of AI should not be limited to text generation. It should help legal teams work across records, images, transcripts, audio, timelines, and other forms of information in ways that support better understanding and better decisions.

For plaintiff firms, this is not merely a technical capability. It can help attorneys, paralegals, case managers, and clients build a clearer shared understanding of complex cases — especially matters involving extensive medical records, long treatment histories, difficult causation questions, and emotionally significant client experiences.

That is what makes Eve especially meaningful to me. It is not merely applying AI to legal work in the abstract. It is building technology around real problems plaintiff firms face every day — problems I have lived inside, worked to improve, and remain deeply motivated to solve.

I would be excited to bring my experience in legal operations, medical-legal communication, workflow adoption, and AI enablement to a company helping firms make technology genuinely useful for the people and clients who depend on it.
